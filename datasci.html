Project 1: Determining how a pitcher's ERA predicts their other statistics Source Code

ERA (Earned run average) is the average number of runs a pitcher gives up over 9 innings (a standard game length). We use https://www.espn.com/mlb/stats/player/_/view/pitching/season/2021/seasontype/2/table/pitching/sort/ERA/dir/asc to find the current top pitcher's ERAs and plot them to visualize the array of ERAs we see:

This graph doesn't tell us anything particularly insightful; however, it shows the different ERAs that the top pitchers in MLB possess.

Another statistic attributed to a pitcher is called wins. This may be self explanatory but a win is given to a starting pitcher if they pitch at least 5 innings and their team wins the game. (A relief pitcher can also obtain a win but the semantics are different). The statistic of wins has gotten a bad reputation recently due to it's inability to predict a pitcher's true ability. The team behind the pitcher has a lot of the outcome of the pitcher obtaining a win which shows it may not have good predictive power of the pitcher's ability. The following graph shows how a pitcher's ERA has little outcome over the number of wins they are awarded (and therefore why wins may not be a helpful statistic):

Clearly, ERA doesn't acurately predict the number of wins a pitcher has. The correlation coefficient is 0.2 which shows low predictive power.

The last statistic we will look to see if ERA predicts well is WHIP (Walks and hits per innings pitched). Basically, this statistic tells how many times they walk or give up a hit to a batter in each inning on average. Intuitively, we would think that if we have a low number of walks and hits per inning, we should expect the pitcher to give up less runs:

With a correlation coefficient of 0.73, we see WHIP is a much better predictor of a pitcher's ability than wins.

All of this analysis is taken by assuming that ERA is a good predictor. Overall, it is considered a strong predictor of the pitcher's abilities; however, a poor defense behind the pitcher may lead to more men on base and therefore more runs given up by the pitcher. A future project can look at FIP (Fielder independent pitching) which examines a pitcher's ERA with a league average defense behind them.

Project 2: Which Launch Angle yields the most Home Runs? Source Code

Launch angle is defined as the angle that the baseball leaves the bat. This can be anywhere from -90 degrees to +90 degrees (stright down and straight up) with 0 degrees being defined as directly horizontal to the ground. We use Statcast data to visualize which angle yields the highest amount of homeruns using https://baseballsavant.mlb.com/statcast_hit_probability?year=2021&type=la Note that we are using current data from the 2021 season only. There is data back to 2015 so a further project could analyze if this best launch angle is consistent among each year. We scrape the data from Statcast using Selenium due to the data being written in Javascript to update daily and import this data into a Pandas dataframe. From here, we use Seaborn to graph the total home runs as a function of launch angle and see a max number of home runs between 27 - 29 degrees.

This roughly fits a Gaussian normal distribution which is not out of the ordinary.
